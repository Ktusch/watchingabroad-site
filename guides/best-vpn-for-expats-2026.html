What Expats Should Look For in a VPN

Not all VPNs are built the same — and most review sites evaluate VPNs from a domestic perspective (speed, privacy, P2P support). As an expat, your requirements are different. Here's what actually matters when you're trying to watch home TV abroad.

1. Streaming unblocking ability

This is the #1 reason expats need a VPN. In 2026, streaming services are more aggressive than ever at blocking VPN IP addresses. BBC iPlayer, Netflix, ITVX, and Disney+ all maintain active lists of known VPN server IPs and block them. A VPN that worked in January may stop working in June. We tested each VPN over 14 days to account for these blocks being updated.

2. Speed

Streaming requires consistent bandwidth. For HD streaming you need at least 5 Mbps; for 4K you need 25 Mbps. A good VPN for streaming should maintain at least 70% of your base internet speed. Any slower and you'll notice buffering, especially during peak evening hours.

3. Device support

Many expats watch TV on devices that don't natively support VPN apps — Apple TV, smart TVs, and game consoles. A VPN that offers router-level setup or has a dedicated Smart DNS feature is significantly more useful for expats than one that only works on phones and laptops.

4. Server locations in your home country

To watch BBC iPlayer, you need a UK server. To watch Hulu, you need a US server. To watch DR TV, you need a Danish server. Make sure your VPN has servers in your home country — not all do, and some have very few, which means overcrowded, slow connections.

5. Obfuscation (for restricted countries)

If you're living in the UAE, China, Turkey, or Russia, you need a VPN with obfuscation technology that disguises VPN traffic as normal HTTPS. Without it, your VPN connection will be blocked at the network level. Only a few VPNs do this well.

📌 What to skip

  • Logging policy debates — yes, privacy matters, but if you're just streaming TV, a strict no-log policy is less important than actually getting the stream to work
  • Kill switch — important for torrenting, but irrelevant for streaming video (if the VPN drops, the stream pauses, no harm done)
  • Number of servers — 5,000 servers means nothing if none of them work with BBC iPlayer. Quality and streaming compatibility matter more than raw count

VPN vs Smart DNS for Expats

If you've been researching ways to watch TV abroad, you've probably come across Smart DNS as an alternative to VPN. Which one should you use?

Here's the short version: VPN encrypts and reroutes all traffic; Smart DNS only reroutes streaming traffic. That difference determines which one is right for you.

VPN Smart DNS
Speed Moderate (encryption overhead) Full speed (no encryption)
Privacy Full (IP and traffic hidden) Minimal (only DNS queries rerouted)
Device support Phones, laptops, some routers Every device (Apple TV, Smart TV, console)
Streaming reliability Good (blocks vary by VPN provider) Very good (harder for services to detect)
Setup Install app, click connect Change DNS settings (manual or automatic)

Our recommendation: Use a VPN as your primary tool for watching TV abroad. Use Smart DNS as a backup for devices that don't support VPN (Apple TV, smart TVs, game consoles). Some VPNs like NordVPN (with SmartPlay) and ExpressVPN (with MediaStreamer) combine both in one subscription.

How to Set Up a VPN for Streaming Abroad

Setting up a VPN for expat streaming is straightforward, but there are a few things specific to the expat use case. Here's how to do it step by step.

Step 1: Pick the right subscription plan

Long-term plans offer the best value — 2-year plans typically cost 60-80% less than monthly billing. All major VPNs offer a 30-day money-back guarantee, so there's no risk in trying a long-term plan and cancelling if it doesn't work.

Step 2: Install on the right devices

Install the VPN app on the devices you use most for streaming. If you're watching on a laptop or phone, just install the native app. For Apple TV, you'll need a VPN that offers Smart DNS (NordVPN SmartPlay or ExpressVPN MediaStreamer) — follow our Apple TV VPN guide for details.

Step 3: Connect to a server in your home country

To watch BBC iPlayer, connect to a UK server. For Netflix US, connect to a US server. Most VPN apps show server lists with flags and city names. Pick a server that's not showing as "high load" for the best experience.

Step 4: Clear your streaming app cache

Streaming apps cache your location. Before testing your VPN, force-close the app and reopen it. On browsers, clear your cookies or use a private/incognito window. This prevents the app from using your cached location instead of the VPN-enabled one.

Step 5: Test and optimise

Open your streaming service. If it works, you're done. If it doesn't, try a different server in the same country — some servers get blocked by streaming services faster than others. If nothing works, contact your VPN's support (all major VPNs have 24/7 live chat).

Pro tip: If you're moving between countries frequently (travelling expat or digital nomad), set up a VPN on your router. This way every device that connects to your home Wi-Fi is automatically protected — no per-device setup needed. Router VPN guides are available from most VPN providers' support sites.

Limitations of VPNs for Expats

VPNs are the best tool for watching TV abroad — but they're not perfect. Here's what you should know before relying on one.

Streaming services are actively blocking VPNs

BBC iPlayer, Netflix, and Disney+ all invest heavily in detecting and blocking VPN traffic. This is an arms race — as VPNs improve their obfuscation, streaming services improve their detection. A VPN that works today may stop working next month. The top VPNs (NordVPN, ExpressVPN) generally restore service within 24-48 hours when this happens.

Speed loss is real

Even the best VPNs reduce your internet speed by 10-20%. On a 100 Mbps connection, that's fine. On a slow hotel or AirBnB connection (< 20 Mbps), speed loss can make streaming unwatchable. Smart DNS doesn't have this problem because it doesn't encrypt traffic.

Some devices don't support VPNs

Apple TV, LG/WebOS smart TVs, Samsung Tizen TVs, and game consoles don't support VPN apps. You either need a router-level VPN setup, a travel router, or a VPN with Smart DNS support. Our Apple TV guide covers this in detail.

Not all countries are equal

Some home countries are harder to find servers for than others. If you're a Danish expat wanting to watch DR TV or TV2 Play, you'll find fewer Danish servers than UK or US ones. Check server availability before committing to a VPN for a specific country.

Free VPNs won't work for streaming

Free VPNs typically don't support streaming — and the ones that claim to are usually too slow, have data caps, or sell your data. Shell out for a paid VPN. At $2.50-4.00/month, it's less than a coffee and gives you access to your home TV.

⚠️ What about VPNs that advertise "streaming" features?

  • Most "streaming-optimised" servers are just regular servers with a label — they don't offer better performance, they're just pre-tested to work with specific streaming services
  • Browser extensions only protect browser traffic, not your streaming apps. Always install the full desktop/mobile app
  • Split tunnelling (routing only streaming traffic through the VPN) is useful for expats who want local services to use their local IP — NordVPN and Surfshark support this

Frequently Asked Questions

Will a VPN let me watch BBC iPlayer abroad?

Yes — if you pick the right VPN. NordVPN, ExpressVPN, Surfshark, and CyberGhost all unblock BBC iPlayer from abroad consistently. Mullvad and many free VPNs don't. The key is choosing a UK server that hasn't been blacklisted by the BBC.

Can I use one VPN for all my devices?

Most VPNs have a device limit (5-10 devices). Surfshark and IPVanish offer unlimited connections. For households with many devices, a router-level VPN setup is the better approach — one VPN connection protects every device on your network.

Is there a free VPN that works for expats?

Proton VPN's free tier is the only free VPN we'd recommend — and even then, it doesn't support streaming. For reliable streaming abroad, budget at least $2-4/month for a paid VPN. It's worth it.

What's the difference between a VPN and Smart DNS?

A VPN encrypts all your internet traffic and routes it through a server in another country. Smart DNS only intercepts DNS queries for streaming services — it's faster but doesn't protect your privacy. Read our full VPN vs Smart DNS guide for the details.

Do I need a VPN if I already have Smart DNS?

Smart DNS is great for devices that don't support VPNs (Apple TV, smart TVs), but it doesn't work for all streaming services. Some platforms — especially newer ones — are better at blocking DNS-based unblocking. A VPN is more reliable overall. Many expats use both: a VPN for their main streaming device and Smart DNS as a backup.

Which VPN works best for Netflix abroad?

NordVPN and ExpressVPN are the most reliable for unblocking Netflix UK and Netflix US. Netflix is the most aggressive streaming service at blocking VPNs — they block thousands of VPN IPs daily. The VPNs at the top of this list invest the most in staying ahead of Netflix's blocks.

Bottom line: A good VPN is the single most effective tool for an expat who wants to watch their home TV abroad. NordVPN is our top pick for most people — it's fast, reliable, unblocks every major streaming service, and offers Smart DNS for devices that need it. If you're on a tight budget or have many devices, Surfshark at $2.49/month is an excellent alternative. And if you're in a country that blocks VPNs, ExpressVPN is worth every penny.
